Trust but Verify

The usage and benefits of large language models (e.g. ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, etc.) remind me of the P vs NP problem.

Large language models (LLMs) are excellent at rapidly generating suggestions for, let's say, the name of the next big thingTM that you are working on.

I, as a mere human being, have a very finite amount of brain power that is quickly sapped by these kinds of creation tasks. But choosing a good name from a list is much easier, and this strategy was common long before the advent of LLMs. For example, an advertising creative director might ask their employees to brainstorm 25 different taglines for a new product.

This loosely mirrors the P vs NP problem.

Generating the right solution is hard (NP), but verifying the right solution is easy (P).

From this perspective I think LLMs have great potential to lessen our cognitive burden for certain tasks.

But on the other hand, perhaps it's still important to teach and practice some of these generative skills on our own, such as writing or coding.

As a personal example, I've used Claude to quickly prototype a basic web application. But when discussing the project with others, I found I had a poor understanding of the codebase. I realized that when learning or doing something new, I should first try it on my own. Once you are an expert, then you avoid this downside. However, I've found that for specialized expert tasks, LLMs are currently a bit lacking. This makes sense since LLMs can only do things well that are in the training set.

It will be interesting to see how the usage of LLMs continues to develop, and whether there will be any educational downsides.
